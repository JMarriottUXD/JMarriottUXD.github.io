Self-service help

Redefining HL's online help approach to better support clients and reduce pressure on the Helpdesk.

What we launched

hl.co.uk/help

Introduction

Our strategic discovery work identified self-service help as a significant client pain point and opportunity to create a more sustainable service model in the face of increasing Helpdesk costs.

To deliver a truly effective solution, we needed to get the business thinking in terms of a holistic help service, rather than help as a collection of FAQ articles and phone lines. As the User Researcher, I worked closely with our Product Manager, UX Designer, Content Designer and development team to build a deep understanding of our clients needs when seeking help.

Engaging the business

A vision workshop with company directors built understanding of HL’s contact strategy and set expectations of how different help scenarios should be supported in the future. This enabled us to define the key foundational pieces required now and to align with wider strategy.

Framing the discussion around different types of help allowed us to understand how directors viewed HL's future approach to handling client contact

Alongside these future-looking conversations, we spent time with Helpdesk agents to understand what clients currently contact about. Coupled with a review of our analytics data, we were able to identify some of the likely pinch points in the existing service. These sessions also provided insight into common user tasks and the type of language clients use when talking about their accounts; a valuable input to the IA work that was to follow.

Workshop discussions with Helpdesk agents

Thousands of FAQs

An in-depth review of the existing solution revealed a service lacking an underlying system or owner; resulting in over 1000 unique FAQs arranged without any clear organising principles. This meant it was almost impossible for users to find the information that would address their issue.

Our content-audit and traffic analysis revealed:

  • The smallest amount of content drives the most engagement. For example, 'How to...' FAQs make up 26% of content but account for 46% of page visits
  • Content was grouped inconsistently, both by product and task, causing masses of duplication and users having to second-guess where to look
  • Content was framed around very specific questions, resulting in lots of individual articles that all essentially covered the same points

We hypothesised that HL's decision to frame Help content as questions led to massive duplication of content, making it harder for clients to find the information they need.

Based on our content audit, we began to explore different models that might better aid client understanding and improve findability.

Information architecture and journey exploration

IA is the foundation of every good service, and especially true for serving clients who need help. The solution lies not in complex multi-page journeys or sophisticated interaction design, but simply to help users find information.

A benchmark tree-test study provided quantitative data on the poor performance of the Help IA for 40 common client tasks.

66% of clients were unable to find the information needed to address their Help query

To raise awareness of the current issues, I put together a 10 minute summary video that enabled colleagues to get up to speed with our findings at a time that worked for them.

"​​Love this way of sharing – very easy to consume and also it’s quite nice to be able to pause and reflect as you’re stepping through it!"

Ed Walsh (Lead Product Manager)

Based on our treejack findings and research to date, I facilitated a series of collaborative IA workshops with colleagues from across the organisation to ideate on potential solutions, which we then explored and refined as a squad into a set of structures to re-test. Over the course of several iterations, we defined a new structure that performed, on average, 31% better than the existing solution.

Crucially, within that overall improvement we achieved huge gains in the findability of content that relates to high call drivers. For example, tasks related to trading queries had success scores of 93%, an increase of 85% from the existing help structure.

Tree-testing allowed us to compare proposed new structures against our benchmark, revealing big improvements in the findability of content for key tasks

Our IA work subsequently informed a restructure of the Helpdesk teams to better reflect the tasks our clients call about, allowing agents to more quickly and effectively resolve client queries. This will also enable continuity between web and phone channels, resulting in a more cohesive cross-channel client experience.

Facilitating an IA workshop

After defining the main structure of Help, we ran a 3 week period of rapid ideation, research and iteration.

I worked closely with the team to define our research goals, which in turn informed the prototypes and stimuli used to facilitate discussion during the interviews.

Early sessions focused on the discovery process clients go through when attempting to find a piece of content. We explored various approaches to this, including conveying the type of content via it’s presentation, stepped triage and a chronological presentation.

Research also enabled us to test and validate our content model and content types.

Defining our research questions, preparing research stimuli and headline findings

Taking these findings, we pulled out the principles from our disposable prototypes that would inform our final solution. We presented these as models to guide our conversations as the team moved into the next design and development phase. One of the guiding principles we put in place at this early stage was ‘less is more’; to prioritise structure over style to get out of the clients’ way when finding help.

 Models helped us frame and communicate the principles behind the help solution

The help solution

Informing the future direction of Help content

Whilst the new structure and journeys were a big improvement, it was clear that the content itself would also have to evolve. We mapped the many existing FAQs against the new content types and grouped them around common topics to solve duplication.

Over the course of the re-write, we took the new content into research to gauge client comprehension of the information. This insight was then fed back into the content workflow.

Delivery

We created a user-story map to prioritise the delivery solution inline with clients' help activities and tasks.

This map focused us on delivering viable journeys as we moved through component structure definition, UI design and development.
‍
With the focus on structure, the foundations of the design solution relied on us developing sound typography and spacing rules to aid content comprehension. Using atomic design methodology, our components are reusable and provide foundations for the wider site. This results in consistency for users and development efficiencies.

Our story map ensured our delivery activities were prioritised around client tasks

Measuring performance
