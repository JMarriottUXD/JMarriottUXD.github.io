Crown Court Billing Alpha

Exploring alternative service models for Crown Court Billing to reduce provider (solicitors/barristers) and caseworker effort, address systemic inefficiencies across the billing workflow, and generate evidence to inform decisions about future process and technology changes.

My Role

Senior User Researcher leading the research approach across Alpha.
I consolidated all existing insight, mapped the end-to-end service, facilitated prioritisation with operational teams, shaped hypotheses with the digital team, and led rapid testing with providers, caseworkers, LAA stakeholders and partner organisations such as the CPS.

Context

The Crown Court Billing service is complex, policy-driven and supported by legacy systems that create significant friction for both solicitors/barristers (providers) and LAA caseworkers. Despite years of research, insight was fragmented, making it difficult to understand the full service picture or prioritise improvements.
At the time of this work:

  • caseworkers were operating at 130% capacity, causing delays and backlog
  • providers were experiencing slow, error-prone claim processes
  • wider policy changes were on the horizon, increasing the need for a clear, evidence-based direction
  • industrial action among barristers highlighted the urgency of improving the provider experience (even though remuneration levels were outside the scope of digital change)

How I Led The Work

  • Consolidated 65+ hours of past research into a usable service view, creating maps that clarified actors, processes, pain points and system dependencies, and refined these with Caseworker Team Leads to capture operational realities and prioritise issues.
  • Identified two core problem statements; one for providers (high effort, errors, duplication) and one for caseworkers (disconnected systems, manual verification), to focus the Alpha on the most impactful opportunities.
  • Ran team sessions to form and evaluate hypotheses, assessing value, risk and technical complexity, and selecting three for Alpha exploration.
  • Led rapid testing of hypotheses using lightweight prototyping: describing “new world” workflows and having caseworkers demonstrate how they would assess claims under new assumptions. This enabled testing of service logic without the overhead of designing new interfaces.
  • Engaged providers, caseworkers, LAA stakeholders and partner organisations (e.g. CPS) through interviews, surveys and collaborative sessions to understand behavioural impacts, data constraints and operational feasibility.

Outcomes

  • Evidence-led decision not to pursue the most ambitious proposed model, bringing clarity and closing down repeated discussions that lacked grounding in real-world feasibility.
  • Validated more incremental improvements that offered meaningful benefits for both providers and caseworkers and were prioritised into the product roadmap.
  • Raised awareness of provider and caseworker challenges across LAA Digital, feeding into strategic planning and resourcing for related billing initiatives.
